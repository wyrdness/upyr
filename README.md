## Overview

Upyr (Old East Slavic упырь; Polish upiór, wąpierz; Ukrainian упир; Russian упырь) is the Slavic name for a class of revenant — a person who, after death, rises from the grave to feed on the living. The earliest known attestation of the word in writing is the Old East Slavic text colophon of the so-called 'Book of Prophets', dated 1047, which names a scribe 'Upir' Lichyj' ('the Wicked Upir'). The 11th–13th-century anti-pagan homily Slovo svyatogo Grigoriya ('Word of Saint Grigoriy') condemns Slavs who 'sacrifice to upyri'. The traditional folkloric upyr is not the pale aristocratic vampire of 19th-century literature but a bloated, ruddy corpse with long fingernails and matted hair, drinking the blood of relatives and spreading disease. Many anti-vampire practices recorded by ethnographers — staking the body, decapitation, prone (face-down) burial, placing iron, garlic, hawthorn, or poppy seeds in the grave, and burning the corpse — are documented archaeologically in Slavic 'deviant burials' from the medieval to early modern periods. The 18th-century 'vampire epidemics' of Habsburg Serbia (notably the Petar Blagojević and Arnold Paole cases of the 1720s–30s) carried the Serbian vampir into German and English news reports, giving the modern Western world its word for the creature.

## Etymology

- **Origin**: Proto-Slavic *ǫpyrь / *ǫpirь, of debated origin. Major hypotheses: a native Slavic root linked either to 'to thrust / to bite' or to a privative form ('not burned'); alternatively a Turkic loan from a word group including ubir / ubyr meaning 'witch, vampire' attested in Tatar and other Turkic languages.
- **Meaning**: Disputed; commonly glossed as 'biter', 'thruster', or via the Turkic borrowing 'witch / blood-drinker'.
- **First use**: Old East Slavic 'Upir' Lichyj' colophon (1047 CE); 'Slovo svyatogo Grigoriya' (11th–13th centuries)

## Distribution

Upyr traditions are found across the Slavic world and adjacent regions, from East Slavic (Russian, Ukrainian, Belarusian) through West Slavic (Polish, Czech, Slovak, Sorbian) to South Slavic (Serbian, Bulgarian, Macedonian) cultures. Closely related traditions exist among Romanians (strigoi), Greeks (vrykolakas), Albanians (shtriga), and Turkic-speaking peoples (ubir).

## Notable Sightings

### Petar Blagojević case ⭐
> **Date**: 1725-07-01  
> **Location**: Village of Kisilova (Kisiljevo), Habsburg Military Frontier, Serbia  
> **Witnesses**: 0 (Habsburg imperial provisor (Frombald), Local villagers)  
Petar Blagojević, a peasant said to have died at age 62, allegedly returned to his village and over eight days nine other villagers fell ill and died. Frombald, the Habsburg provisor, accompanied villagers to the exhumation; the body was reported uncorrupted with fresh blood at the mouth, was staked, and burned.
**Credibility**: low — One of the cases that introduced the Serbian 'vampir' to Western Europe.
**Sources**: Frombald report (1725); Wienerisches Diarium (1725)

### Arnold Paole case ⭐
> **Date**: 1731-12-01  
> **Location**: Village of Medveđa, Habsburg Military Frontier, Serbia  
> **Witnesses**: 0 (Habsburg imperial medical commission (Glaser, Flückinger), Local villagers)  
Arnold Paole, a hajduk soldier, reportedly fell from a hay-wagon and died around 1726. After his death, villagers blamed him for further deaths and exhumed and burned the body. In 1731 a fresh outbreak in Medveđa prompted Habsburg medical officer Johann Flückinger and colleagues to investigate; their Visum et Repertum (1732) described 12 of 17 exhumed bodies as 'in a vampire condition'.
**Credibility**: low — The case that set off Western Europe's 'vampire controversy' of the 1730s and gave Calmet his major case study.
**Sources**: Flückinger, J. et al., Visum et Repertum (1732); Calmet, A. (1746)

## Theories & Explanations

### Paranormal / Cryptozoological

**Genuine revenant** _(Slavic folk tradition)_

Within Slavic folk belief, the upyr is a real revenant: the soul of the improperly buried, cursed, or unbaptized dead returning bodily to the world.

### Skeptical

**Misinterpretation of normal decomposition** _(Augustin Calmet (1746, partly), Gerard van Swieten (1755), Paul Barber (1988, Vampires, Burial, and Death))_

Most signs taken as evidence of vampirism — bloating, ruddy color, fresh-looking blood at the mouth, apparent growth of nails and hair — are explained by normal post-mortem changes (gas accumulation, skin retraction, decomposition pooling). Augustin Calmet (1746) and Gerard van Swieten (1755) reached this conclusion in the Enlightenment.

**Vampire scares track epidemic disease** _(Paul Barber, Modern epidemiological folklore studies)_

Outbreaks of vampire belief tend to cluster with epidemics, particularly tuberculosis and rabies, and with periods of communal stress.

**Premature burial** _(Various 19th- and 20th-century commentators)_

Cases of apparent revenance may sometimes have arisen from accidental burial of catatonic or deeply ill persons before death, who awoke in the grave.
